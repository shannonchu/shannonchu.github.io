TENSOS

Product, packaging & Graphic design


In my Product Development class, I worked with a multi-disciplinary team of engineering and business students to explore human-centered design opportunities in developing a toy tensegrity to be used for educational outreach and to foster interest in STEM fields.

(Note: we changed the product name to TENSOS after we had already constructed our final prototype packaging, which is why it says Tensegrity Kit instead of TENSOS.)

What is tensegrity?

Put simply, a tensegrity is a 3-dimensional structure consisting of rigid members that don't touch and are suspended by a web of cords under tension. A tensegrity structure has a dynamic of basically being stretched apart and pulled together at the same time!

NASA is exploring tensegrity concepts to build the next generation of planetary rovers. This is because tensegrity structures can flexibly move and respond to changes in surrounding, which could allow for more resilient and reliable spacecrafts.

Our mission

We set out to provide K-8 school children an affordable and engaging toy that allows them to explore basic scientific concepts and learn about current NASA research and how it is being applied to explore the galaxy. We wanted our product to help nurture a fascination for science in kids at an early age so that they pursue STEM related careers in the future.

The process

As this was for a product development class, we went through the entire product development lifecycle. We had to design our product with two different demographics in mind: our target customers (the parents and teachers purchasing our product), and our end users (children). It was a special challenge tackling this dual nature to create a product that would appeal to parents but also keep kids engaged.

As a team, we determined customer and user needs, analyzed competing products, and researched related products available in the market. We brainstormed over 120 ideas for our product, which we eventually narrowed down to 3. We conducted business analyses and tested our top 3 designs with potential customers and users, iterating and honing our prototypes based on feedback. After aggregating comments and suggestions, the team decided to merge all three concepts into one overarching design.

The team collectively worked on building the final prototype and business plan. Luckily, we had team members from a variety of backgrounds including mechanical engineering, business, design, and cognitive science. Everyone's skills and expertise shone when executing the final product.

I was primarily responsible for designing all of the product visuals. This included the brand identity, packaging, and instruction manual. I created designs that would excite and engage kids and that would work across various mediums (print on paper versus laser cut into wood). It was especially exciting designing the packaging because it was my first time working with laser cutters and had to design with laser cutter restrictions in mind.

The product

TENSOS is a fun, affordable, and interactive toy building kit for K-8 children that teaches about tensegrity, NASA, and how to build your own tensegrity toy. TENSOS generates interest in STEM fields through building and playing!

The TENSOS building kit comes with all the materials needed to build your own tensegrity model and an instruction manual complete with colorful and clean visuals, kid-friendly instructions on how to assemble various tensegrity models, and background information to educate users on tensegrity concepts and how NASA is utilizing tensegrity for space exploration.

TENSOS also creates a community for children to expand their building and science knowledge with other excited kids. We have a website that acts like a forum where children can share their designs with others around the world. There, kids can also find supplementary step-by-step video instructions for the various tensegrity models that are covered in the instruction manual.

At the end of the semester, each team pitched their products to campus community members, faculty, and industry judges in a final tradeshow. Our team was lucky enough to speak with and show our final product to a member of the NASA tensegrity team!
